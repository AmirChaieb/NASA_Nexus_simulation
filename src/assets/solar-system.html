MOON

Description

THE MOON!

Natural satellite of Earth

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite and the fifth-largest moon in the Solar System. It is unique as it plays a crucial role in stabilizing Earth's axial tilt, which helps maintain a stable climate. The Moon is also responsible for tides on Earth due to its gravitational pull.

The Moon’s surface is marked by craters, mountains, and flat plains called "maria" formed by ancient volcanic activity. It has no atmosphere to protect it from meteoroids, which is why its surface is heavily cratered. The Moon lacks liquid water, but recent studies have detected small amounts of ice in permanently shadowed craters near its poles.

Surface Temperature

Ranges from -173°C to 127°C (-280°F to 260°F).

Atmosphere

Extremely thin, almost negligible (exosphere).

Moon Facts
Interesting Facts

Facts

The Moon is slowly moving away from Earth at a rate of about 3.8 cm (1.5 inches) per year. It always shows the same face to Earth because its rotational period matches its orbital period, a phenomenon known as tidal locking. The far side of the Moon, often mistakenly called the "dark side," receives sunlight but remains hidden from Earth's view.

Jupiter

Description

Gas giant

Jupiter is the largest planet in the Solar System and is primarily composed of hydrogen and helium. It is known for its prominent Great Red Spot, a massive storm larger than Earth that has been raging for centuries. Jupiter has a very thick atmosphere with complex weather patterns, including bands of clouds and high-speed winds.

The planet has a strong magnetic field and is surrounded by a large number of moons, with 79 confirmed as of 2021. Four of these moons—Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto—are known as the Galilean moons, discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610.

Jupiter Facts
Key Facts

Facts

Rings: Jupiter has a faint ring system composed of dust particles and small rocks, primarily made from material ejected by its moons.
Moons: Ganymede, one of Jupiter's moons, is the largest moon in the Solar System and is larger than the planet Mercury. Europa is of particular interest to scientists due to its subsurface ocean, which may harbor conditions suitable for life.
Exploration: Jupiter has been explored by several spacecraft, including Pioneer, Voyager, Galileo, and more recently, the Juno spacecraft, which is currently studying the planet's atmosphere, magnetic field, and gravitational field.

Mars

Description

Mars

Terrestrial planet

Mars, often referred to as the "Red Planet," is the fourth planet from the Sun and is known for its reddish appearance, which is due to iron oxide (rust) on its surface. The planet has the largest volcano in the Solar System, Olympus Mons, and a massive canyon system, Valles Marineris, which is over 4,000 km long.

Mars has a thin atmosphere composed mainly of carbon dioxide, with traces of nitrogen and argon. This thin atmosphere contributes to the planet's extreme temperature fluctuations and makes it less effective at retaining heat. Mars experiences seasons similar to Earth due to its axial tilt, but they last about twice as long due to its longer orbital period.

Mars Facts
Key Facts

Facts

The Moon is slowly moving away from Earth at a rate of about 3.8 cm (1.5 inches) per year. Moons: Mars has two small moons, Phobos and Deimos, which are thought to be captured asteroids.
Water: Evidence suggests that liquid water once flowed on Mars, and there are polar ice caps composed of water and carbon dioxide ice. Recent missions have detected signs of briny liquid water flowing on the surface during warmer months.
Exploration: Mars has been a major focus of robotic exploration, with missions including the Mars rovers (like Spirit, Opportunity, Curiosity, and Perseverance) and orbiters (such as Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter and MAVEN), providing valuable data about the planet’s geology, climate, and potential for past life.

SUN

Description

AHOY SUN

Our Solar System's Star

The Sun is a G-type main-sequence star at the center of our Solar System, crucial for providing light and energy. With a diameter of about 864,000 miles, it constitutes roughly 99.86% of the Solar System's mass. Primarily composed of hydrogen and helium, the Sun generates energy through nuclear fusion in its core, reaching temperatures of about 27 million °F (15 million °C). Its surface, or photosphere, has a temperature of around 10,000 °F (5,500 °C).

Sun Facts
Key Facts

Facts

Huge Size: It makes up over 99.8% of the Solar System’s mass, and over a million Earths could fit inside it.
Intense Energy: The Sun releases immense energy every second, enough to power human civilization for 500,000 years.
Hot Core: The core temperature reaches around 27 million°F (15 million°C), while its surface is about 10,000°F (5,500°C).
Solar Wind: The Sun emits charged particles (solar wind), creating the heliosphere, a bubble that envelops the Solar System.
Life Cycle: The Sun is about halfway through its life, with about 5 billion years left before it becomes a red giant.
